PROCESS:

The fabric to be printed is pretreated to remove the starch and the dust contains on it. The running material is then spread and dried completely depending on the weather conditions. After the material is completely dried it is spread on the printing table and fixed tightly with the pins. The portions to be printed are clearly marked with the help of a scale and chalk.

Separate blocks are used for each colour to be highlighted with the designs of the motifs. After the printing paste is spread on the wooden tray the designs are printed on the fabric. For outlines the blocks are tapped and the colour is printed, for intricate designs the hand blocks are pressed hard with fist for printing. After all the colours of the design blocks are imprinted it is steamed and spread on the drying hanger which is above the printing table. It is then either folded or rolled for the further process of cutting or sewing.

Step 1 : Hari Sarana

The dirt and dust from the cloth must be washed off so that the prints are vibrant. The fabric is submerged in a bleach and water solution for a few days and then washed with boiling water.

Step 2 : Marking

The cloth is marked with chalk and scale. This helps with the printing process as the artisan can easily see where the blocks are to be placed

Step 3: Chapai

The process of printing is called Chapaai and is done with the help of the wooden or metal blocks. The cloth is printed from left to right, and the number of blocks to be used depends on the number of colours to be used.

Step 4 : Sukhaai / Drying

Also known as Sukhaai, this process is done to ensure that the colour gets secured on the cloth. This process is especially crucial for pigmented dyes. During this process, the colour may transfer onto other surfaces, and hence, the fabric is covered in plastic or newspaper.

Step 5 : Dhulaai / Washing

The final step in the printing process is Dhulaai, wherein the cloth goes through exclusive boilers and steaming process, which helps fix the colour further into the cloth.
